The Problem

Ever take your canine companion for a nice relaxing day at the beach, spend the afternoon at the local park, or just go out for a quick stroll around the block? If you're a dog owner you might have said yes to more than one of those, and you've probably had at least one of those experiences be less than relaxing; having your dog run up to some kids at the beach and worried the parents will see your sweet Fido as a hellhound and beat him back, or you have had a random dog bolting straight for yours at the park and you can already see the vet bill burning a hole in your wallet or having to pass another pet parent and their pooch pass you on a narrow sidewalk which normally would be fine... But their teacup size pup is starting to look like a chew toy in the eyes of your hound.

Sometimes these outings can lead to heartbreaking incidents that could have been avoided, either someone assumes that their dog is friendly and so yours must be too, or your dog is given a bad name for how they look rather than who they are. And while trying to protect someone else's furbaby from yours can be seen as uncalled for hostility, or in some situations a dog might not get to go and have the same experiences like getting to the park because it is the "safer" option or similar compromises.

At the end of the day, the dog's personality is not the problem, miscommunication, and assumptions. All dog owners know their dog, the problem is no one else does. Your dog might be a social butterfly and want to say hello to everyone and every dog, or maybe they play rough and get mislabelled as aggressive, or just need some time to warm up to others... and just like how some people don't like other people, some dogs don't like other dogs, and that's okay.

So having identified communication as the problem, the next issue is how can you tell other pet parents about your pooch's personality without being close enough to sniff one another and not having to bark from across the park?

The Solution!

You're more likely to find someone who owns a smartphone than someone who has a dog, so to tackle our problem of miscommunication a mobile app which allows users to see which dogs are at the park and have a quick read of their personalities and gauge how well your dog will get along with them, or receive a notification of another dog coming around the corner and others can view your profile and know how best to respect your dog's space and time outside.

The purpose of the app is prevention. Being able to clearly see that a dog is "non-friendly" or that they don't play well with smaller children or dogs, or having something able to check your blindspot can make the difference between life and death, in some cases. To better explain all this I have created a demo of how the app should look and function in more detail which you can find in the How does it work section.

About (Me)

At this point, you might be wondering why I have chosen to help the canine community with this issue.

My background is a bit peculiar working with Geographical Infomation Systems and also in training service dogs, so this project is a blending of the two fields. But the real inspiration for this app came from thinking back to when I worked at the KSIA in Durban, South Africa as an explosive-detection dog handler with my partner, K9 Diesel, a massive male Dutch Shepherd who was trained for both detection and apprehension.

He was a big boy with a bigger bite... but also a sucker for attention. He would love to say hello to everyone working at the airline check-in counters, but because of his breed and size, most would panic and try to scare him off. Labeled as aggressive based solely on his appearance, no one took into consideration that as a certified working dog he had been trained to do well around new people. However, on our days off when I would take him to the beach, it was the total opposite. Many people would walk their dogs off-leash and let them run up to strangers and other dogs. This terrified me as Diesel was not fond of other dogs, especially small ones. I always had a leash on him, but a leash doesn't do much for the other dog running free.

After reading the briefing and looking for "someone" to help, Diesel and other dogs like him seemed to be the perfect fit. And my coding background was purely from working with GIS and ESRI products such as ArcGIS in Python. Which after these three weeks, I realized was very limited and I depended heavily on the tools ESRI provided. So for the application and reading in the emails how important it is to learn rather than just showcase existing abilities I decided to try to learn how to program in JavaScript, build a webpage in HTML and CSS and create a functional map without ArcGIS. This website and the demo included are very basic and somewhat primitive, but I hope you can see potential in the work presented to you and appreciate the project for what it is as well as what it can and will be.
